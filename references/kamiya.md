# Mieko Kamiya — The Seven Needs

## Who She Was

**Mieko Kamiya (神谷美恵子, 1914–1979)** — Japanese psychiatrist, translator, essayist. Worked at the Nagashima Aisei-en leprosarium in the Seto Inland Sea. Her 1966 book *Ikigai ni Tsuite* (*On the Meaning of Life*, 生きがいについて) is the foundational scholarly text on ikigai. She is widely called "the mother of ikigai psychology."

A critical observation from her clinical work, central to everything that follows: **symptom severity did not correlate with ikigai**. Mildly affected patients sometimes felt purposeless; severely affected patients sometimes reported profound ikigai-kan. The feeling is not a function of circumstance; it is a function of attention, relation, and meaning.

## Kamiya's Six Characteristics of Ikigai

1. It produces ikigai-kan (the felt sense that life is worth living).
2. It does not relate to practical benefit or utility.
3. It is intrinsically motivated — "I do it because I want to."
4. It is **radically individual** — cannot be borrowed, imitated, or transplanted.
5. It creates a personal value system.
6. It forms a unique inner mental space.

Consequence for the skill: do not offer the user someone else's ikigai as a suggestion. "Have you considered gardening?" is wrong. You can ask about gardening in the context of *their* life, but not prescribe it.

## The Seven Needs (from *Ikigai ni Tsuite*)

Kamiya identified seven human needs that ikigai fulfills. Each can serve as both a diagnostic lens (which needs does my current life meet? which are thin?) and a direction for attention.

### 1. Life Satisfaction
A sense that life is moving toward a better state. People meet this differently: extroverts through active pursuits and relationships; introverts through private hobbies and inner work. Kamiya cautioned that negative emotions — fear, resentment, envy — actively undermine this need.

**Diagnostic prompt:** "Is your life, overall, moving in a direction that feels right to you? Not perfect — but right-ish?"

### 2. Change and Growth
Humans require novelty. Kamiya: "a sense of fulfilment is closely linked to the desire for change." Stagnation and boredom are signals of poor mental health, not virtuous stability.

**Diagnostic prompt:** "In the last six months, what have you learned, become, or changed into? If the answer is nothing, sit with that for a moment."

### 3. A Bright Future
Optimism about life's trajectory. Kamiya advocated pursuing **both** short-term achievable goals and long-term ambitions. The short-term ones generate ikigai-kan week to week; the long-term ones provide backbone during hardship.

**Diagnostic prompt:** "What are you looking forward to in the next week? In the next five years?"

### 4. Resonance
The drive for meaningful social connection — feeling one's actions "connect with one's surroundings." Kamiya identified love and dedication to significant others as primary satisfactions. Not audiences. Not followers. Specific people.

**Diagnostic prompt:** "Who in your life would notice if you were gone? Who would you notice if they were gone? Not theoretically — specifically."

### 5. Freedom
"Indispensable as air." Choice, agency, the ability to direct one's own life. Crucially — Kamiya recognized that **freely chosen constraints still express freedom.** A parent staying home, a monk taking vows, a caregiver sacrificing flexibility — these are not failures of freedom if freely chosen. Pay attention to whether the user is experiencing their constraints as chosen or imposed.

**Diagnostic prompt:** "What in your life did you choose? What was chosen for you? How do you feel about each?"

### 6. Self-Actualization
A unique mission "that only they can do." This is narrower than the Maslow version — Kamiya championed creativity in **modest** pursuits: cooking, parenting, craft. She considered the act of creating itself as confirming life's meaning, regardless of the output's grandeur.

**Diagnostic prompt:** "What is one thing you make — meal, letter, object, experience — that is recognizably *yours*, that no one else would quite make the same way?"

### 7. Meaning and Value
The deepest need: the willingness to ask "what am I living for?" and "is life worth living?" Notably, Kamiya taught that the **reflection itself** matters more than having a crisp answer. The practice is the point.

**Diagnostic prompt:** "When you ask yourself 'is my life worth living?' — honestly, not performatively — what comes up?"

## The Seven Needs Audit (Session 5 exercise)

Ask the user to rate each of the seven needs from **1 (very thin) to 5 (well met)** based on their current life. Then:

1. Identify the 2 strongest needs — these are likely where their ikigai is already concentrated. Ask what specific people, practices, or things correspond to those strengths. These go in the journal as **active ikigai sources**.
2. Identify the 2 weakest needs. Do not declare them problems. Ask: "is there anything about that reading that surprises you?" Some thinness is chosen; some is drift.
3. For the 2 weakest, invite the user to name **one modest, concrete practice** they could add in the next week. Not a life overhaul. Kamiya was emphatic that creation in modest pursuits counts.

This is the authentic Japanese alternative to the four-circle Venn exercise. Use it in Session 5, and revisit it in the post-Session-7 synthesis.

## A Note on Language

Kamiya writes in Japanese about 欲求 (*yokkyū*, "desire/need"). Translations vary — the seven are sometimes rendered as:

| English (this doc) | Alternate renderings |
|---|---|
| Life satisfaction | A sense of life progressing |
| Change and growth | Growth, novelty |
| A bright future | Optimism, a hopeful horizon |
| Resonance | Echo, connection, influence |
| Freedom | Autonomy, choice |
| Self-actualization | Self-fulfilment, unique mission |
| Meaning and value | Meaning of life, worth |

All are correct-ish. Use whichever language lands for the user.
