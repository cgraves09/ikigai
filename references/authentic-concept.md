# What Ikigai Actually Is

## Etymology

**Ikigai** (生き甲斐) = *iki* (生き, "life / living / alive") + *kai* (甲斐, "worth / value / effect"). When voiced in compound form, *kai* becomes *gai*. Literal gloss: "the value of living" or "that which makes life worth living."

The *kai* element traces to the Heian period (794–1185), when aristocrats played *kai-awase*, a matching game of hand-decorated shells. The shells were so prized that *kai* came to signify "worth" by association.

## Ikigai Is Two Things at Once

This distinction is the single most important thing to hold onto. Mieko Kamiya — the Japanese psychiatrist who founded ikigai psychology — carefully distinguished between:

1. **Ikigai (object / source)** — the thing itself. Japanese people say: *"This child is my ikigai."* *"My garden is my ikigai."* *"Playing shogi on Sundays is my ikigai."*
2. **Ikigai-kan (生きがい感, the feeling)** — the felt sensation that one's life has value, arising *because of* that object. Kamiya wrote that ikigai-kan, compared with happiness, "has a clearer sense of attitude toward the future" and is "strongly linked with one's personal values."

Western writing almost always collapses these two into one muddy "life purpose." The skill must hold them apart. When a user says "I think my ikigai is X," ask two questions:
- The object: what is X, specifically?
- The feeling: what does it feel like, in your body, when you do X or think about it?

## People Usually Have Many Ikigai

There is no singular ikigai. Japanese people typically list several — family members, a hobby, a morning walk, a craft, a practice. The Western "find your one true purpose" framing is alien to the concept.

## Ohsaki Study — How the Word Was Actually Measured

In the landmark Tohoku University cohort study (Sone et al., 2008) of 43,391 Japanese adults followed for seven years, participants were asked **one question**:

> *"Do you have ikigai in your life?"*

Response options: yes / uncertain / no. Those answering yes had significantly lower all-cause mortality. The researchers did not hand out a Venn diagram. They did not ask "what's your calling." They asked the feeling.

The skill's first-session question mirrors this. Before any framework, ask the user that question literally. Sit with the answer.

## How Japanese People Describe Their Ikigai

Ken Mogi opens *The Little Book of Ikigai* with this definition:

> "Ikigai is the reason you get up in the morning. It could be something very small like having a cup of coffee and a chocolate... or something big, like going to Mars or winning the Nobel Prize."

Japanese government writing (Government of Japan, 2022) describes ikigai as "a broad concept — it refers to that which brings value and joy to life: from people, such as one's children or friends, to activities including work and hobbies."

Ogimi Village (Okinawa) elders, interviewed about their own ikigai, cited: friendships, gardening, artistic pursuits, continued work past retirement, community participation, and small daily rituals.

Mogi's karaoke example is diagnostic: *singing badly can absolutely be your ikigai*, because you love doing it. The Venn diagram ("what you're good at") excludes it. Authentic ikigai includes it.

## Three Types (Inoue's Categorization)

Japanese psychologist Katsuya Inoue classifies ikigai into three descriptive categories:

1. **Social ikigai** — volunteer work, community activities, service.
2. **Non-social ikigai** — faith, self-discipline, private practices, solitary craft.
3. **Anti-social ikigai** — dark motivational drives (e.g., desire for revenge). Included by Japanese researchers because honest description requires it — *any* reliable "reason to keep going" counts as ikigai, even unromantic ones.

The skill should honor this descriptive honesty. Do not only validate Instagram-friendly ikigai.

## Four Practical Buckets (for session use)

Combining Kamiya, Ohsaki, and the Kemp interviews with 100+ Japanese people, these are the practical source-buckets to probe:

- **Relationships** — family, friends, pets, mentors, community.
- **Personal pursuits and hobbies** — craft, art, games, collecting, making.
- **Goals, growth, and learning** — things you are working toward or learning.
- **Small daily joys and rituals** — the morning coffee, the walk, the first sip of tea, a bird at the window.

Bucket 4 is where most Japanese people's ikigai actually lives. Westernized discovery processes systematically underweight it. Don't.

## What Ikigai Is Not

- Not your job (usually).
- Not a single "life purpose" statement.
- Not something you have to achieve or earn.
- Not monetizable by definition.
- Not dependent on excellence (karaoke sung badly still counts).
- Not fixed across your life — it will shift with life stages.
- Not a feeling of constant pleasure; often it is dutiful, repeated attention that produces a quiet ikigai-kan underneath (see Morita therapy in [mogi.md](mogi.md)).
