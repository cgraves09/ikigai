# Why the Four-Circle Venn Diagram Is Wrong

## Where It Actually Came From

The famous diagram — four overlapping circles labeled:
- What you love
- What you're good at
- What the world needs
- What you can be paid for

— with "Ikigai" written in the central intersection, is **not Japanese**.

It was created by British entrepreneur **Marc Winn in 2014**. He took Andrés Zuzunaga's 2011 Spanish-language diagram called "Purpose" and simply replaced the word "Purpose" with "Ikigai." That substitution is the entirety of its Japanese heritage. Wikipedia notes the diagram "does not originate in Japan" and has been described as "catchy but misleading" and "an unrealistic ideal."

The diagram is now so viral that it dominates the first page of Google results for "ikigai." That does not make it correct.

## Ken Mogi's Verdict

Ken Mogi — Japanese neuroscientist, author of *The Little Book of Ikigai*, arguably the most prominent living popularizer of the concept — has addressed the diagram directly in an interview with Nicholas Kemp of Ikigai Tribe:

> "This diagram is very strange because it defines ikigai as the intersection of all these things, what you love, what the world needs and what you can be paid for, and what you are good at… That would be great if you can do something that satisfies all these requirements. But that is too narrow of a definition for ikigai."

> "It's rubbish… this diagram is rubbish. It doesn't have anything to do with the essence of ikigai."

> "The only condition would be this: what you love. This is absolutely necessary condition for ikigai. All these conditions are superfluous. They are non-essential, and then nonsensical from the Japanese point of view."

> "Even if you're not paid for something you do, for example, if you love to paint a picture, and you're not paid for that, that is ikigai, of course."

## What the Diagram Gets Wrong

### 1. It conflates ikigai with career / calling
The Venn diagram is really a diagram about vocation — about finding monetizable meaningful work. That is a legitimate concept. It is not ikigai. Many Japanese people's ikigai has nothing to do with work, which is why the diagram's treatment is fundamentally off-target.

### 2. It requires all four circles to overlap
The diagram implies ikigai only exists where all four conditions are met simultaneously. Mogi: "a single activity can be ikigai, regardless of whether it hits all four or just one criterion." A hobby you aren't good at, will never monetize, and the world doesn't need — karaoke, badly — is still fully ikigai.

### 3. It treats ikigai as a single, singular purpose
Japanese people typically have **many** ikigai at once: children, garden, walk, craft, friend. The singular "find your ikigai" phrasing is already wrong; the singular *central intersection* of the diagram doubles down on the error.

### 4. It erases ikigai-kan (the feeling dimension)
Mieko Kamiya's foundational distinction between ikigai-the-object and ikigai-kan-the-felt-sense has no place in the diagram. The diagram is a static map; authentic ikigai is a living feeling tied to a living practice. See [authentic-concept.md](authentic-concept.md).

### 5. It demands external validation / achievement
"What you're good at" and "what the world needs" are both judged from outside. Kamiya characterized ikigai as *radically private*: it cannot be borrowed, imitated, or externally validated. She taught that "what is important is pursuing their purpose, not whether or not they accomplish their goal."

### 6. It imports the most un-Japanese element
"What you can be paid for" is the single most non-Japanese circle. Mogi explicitly calls the monetization requirement "superfluous" and "nonsensical from the Japanese point of view."

### 7. It ignores small daily things
Where on the diagram do you put "the first sip of tea in the morning"? It hits, at best, one of four circles. Yet this is precisely the kind of thing Japanese people commonly cite as ikigai.

## What to Say If the User Brings Up the Diagram

A plain, non-lecturing response:

> "That diagram's actually not Japanese — a British guy called Marc Winn made it in 2014 by swapping 'Purpose' for 'Ikigai' on someone else's diagram. Ken Mogi, who wrote the bestselling book on ikigai, calls it 'rubbish.' It's really a diagram about finding a lucrative meaningful career, which is a legitimate question but a different question. The Japanese concept is looser: a thing you love that makes your life feel worth living. Your garden counts. Your morning coffee counts. Your bad karaoke counts. The four circles don't have to overlap."

If they want to use the diagram anyway because they find it clarifying for the career question — fine, that's their prerogative, but name what it is: a purpose/vocation diagram with the wrong label. Don't pretend you're helping them find their ikigai by using it.
