# The Ikigai-9 Scale

## What It Is

The Ikigai-9 is a **validated psychometric scale** for measuring ikigai. It was developed and validated on a Japanese population by Imai, Osada, and Nishimura (Tokyo, 2012) and translated and validated for English speakers by Fido and Kotera (University of Derby / University of Nottingham, 2019).

Research on the UK validation found that ikigai, as measured by this scale, predicted greater mental wellbeing and lower depression scores — above and beyond sex and age.

Use it as:
- A **Session 1 baseline** — administer it before starting discovery work.
- A **post-Session 7 re-measure** — administer again after the skill's core sessions. Compare. Do not use the comparison to grade the user; use it to notice the direction of drift.
- An **occasional revisit** — if the user returns months later, administer again.

## The Nine Items

Ask the user to rate each item on a 1–5 scale:

**1 = does not apply to me at all**
**2 = applies a little**
**3 = applies somewhat**
**4 = applies to me**
**5 = applies to me a lot**

The items (English-validated wording from Fido & Kotera 2019):

1. I believe that I have some impact on someone.
2. My life is mentally rich and fulfilled.
3. I am interested in many things.
4. I feel that I am contributing to someone or to society.
5. I would like to develop myself.
6. I often feel that I'm happy.
7. I think that my existence is needed by something or someone.
8. I would like to learn something new or start something.
9. I have room in my mind.

Sum the scores for a total between 9 and 45.

## The Three Factors

The validation study identified three factors. The items roughly group as:

- **Factor 1 — Positive emotions toward one's present life.** Items 2, 6, 9 ("mentally rich," "often happy," "room in my mind").
- **Factor 2 — Active, positive attitudes toward one's future.** Items 3, 5, 8 ("interested in many things," "would like to develop myself," "would like to learn something new").
- **Factor 3 — Acknowledgment of the meaning of one's existence.** Items 1, 4, 7 ("have some impact," "contributing to someone or society," "existence is needed").

Low scores on a specific factor point to different practices:

- **Low Factor 1** → prioritize Mogi's Pillar 4 (Joy of Little Things) and Pillar 5 (Here and Now). The user's attention is not with their life in the present.
- **Low Factor 2** → prioritize Kamiya's need #2 (Change and Growth) and #3 (Bright Future). The user may be stuck or resigned.
- **Low Factor 3** → prioritize Kamiya's need #4 (Resonance) and Mogi's Pillar 3 (Harmony). The user may be disconnected from the specific people or communities where ikigai-kan is generated.

## How to Administer It

In Session 1 and again after Session 7:

1. Introduce: "I'd like to do a short baseline — nine statements. For each, rate how much it applies to you, 1 to 5. There's no right answer. Skip any that don't land and we'll come back. Ready?"
2. Read each item. Let the user answer verbally. You record the number.
3. Don't interpret during the scale — save reflection for after.
4. When all nine are in, calculate: total score, and which factor has the lowest item average.
5. Record in the journal under the session's **Baseline** (first time) or **Re-measure** (final time) section. Keep the individual item scores, not just the total — the item-level detail is useful for the synthesis.

## Interpretive Notes

- **Do not treat the total as a grade.** The scale was developed as a research instrument, not a personal scorecard. The *direction* of change across administrations is more meaningful than the absolute number.
- **A low score is diagnostic, not damning.** If the user scores low, name it plainly and gently: "that's a useful starting point — it tells us where attention is needed." Then go to work.
- **A high score does not mean skip the skill.** Someone can score 40+ and still benefit from the noticing practice. Often people who score high already have strong ikigai and don't know the word for it; the skill gives them the vocabulary and the log.
