# Ken Mogi — The Five Pillars (and Morita Therapy)

## Who He Is

**Ken Mogi (茂木健一郎)** — Japanese neuroscientist, senior researcher at Sony Computer Science Laboratories, popular author. His 2017 *The Little Book of Ikigai* is the most widely-read English-language treatment of ikigai written by a Japanese author.

Mogi's framework is **non-hierarchical and non-exhaustive.** From the book: "The pillars reinforce each other… they are not mutually exclusive or exhaustive, nor do they have a particular order." Treat the pillars as overlapping lenses on the same terrain, not as sequential steps.

## The Five Pillars

### Pillar 1 — Starting Small & Kodawari

Begin at the smallest possible unit. The craftsman refining a single broth for thirty years. The barista adjusting the grind by half a second. **Kodawari** (こだわり) — personal commitment to a detail others may not notice — is the texture of this pillar.

Kodawari is diagnostic: wherever you already show it, there is already ikigai. Ramen masters don't become ramen masters by setting out to "find their purpose." They show up and refine, and the ikigai grows beneath the attention.

**Key move:** Don't start with ambition. Start with one thing, done with more care than strictly necessary.

**Session exercises (for Session 3 Kodawari and Session 7 Tiny Experiments):**
- "Name an activity where you apply more care than anyone would require."
- "Where in your life do you notice quality others might miss?"
- "If no one ever praised or noticed you again, what would you still do with care?"
- "What's the smallest unit of [the thing you feel pulled toward] you could do this week? Smaller. Smaller than that."

### Pillar 2 — Releasing Yourself

Self-acceptance. Letting go of the ego-project, the curated self-image, the need for external validation. Mogi calls this "a low-budget, maintenance-free formula for being happy."

This pillar is about lowering the cost of your own existence. Ikigai is hard to feel when you are constantly managing performance, anxiety about status, or the gap between who you are and who you wish you were.

**Key moves:**
- Notice where you are performing, not living.
- What parts of yourself have you disowned as "not the right kind of person who would do that"?
- Where is self-criticism preventing something modest and good from happening?

This pillar is less about a single exercise and more about tone across the whole practice. The skill must never impose a "you should become" narrative. Ikigai is downstream of self-acceptance, not achievement.

### Pillar 3 — Harmony and Sustainability

Living in balance with others and with the natural world. Not standalone heroism. Mogi: "A man is like a forest, individual yet connected."

This pillar has two edges:
- **Relational** — ikigai is often produced by being interwoven with specific others. Care is an ikigai. Contribution to a small community is an ikigai.
- **Sustainable** — a practice that burns you out or harms those around you cannot be ikigai. Authentic ikigai has a quality of being indefinitely continuable.

**Session exercises:**
- "Who are the three specific people whose presence in your life makes things make sense?"
- "What practice in your life could continue for thirty years without breaking you?"
- "Where does your current pace fight against ikigai?"

### Pillar 4 — The Joy of Little Things

Sensory delight in small pleasures. Mogi's neuroscientific framing: building a habit of beloved small things early in the day primes dopamine release — you begin each day with a "little ikigai."

This is the pillar the Venn diagram cannot see. It is also, empirically, where most Japanese people's ikigai lives.

**Session exercises (Session 2 centerpiece):**
- "Describe your morning ritual. What one detail of it do you actually look forward to?"
- **The 7-day Small Ikigai Log** (carry-exercise after Session 1): each day, note one small thing — two sentences max — that brought you a quiet sense that life was worth living today. The coffee. The light in the hallway. A stranger's smile. The dog on the walk.
- After the log: "Read the seven entries. What pattern do you notice — sensory, relational, solitary, ritual?"

### Pillar 5 — Being in the Here and Now

Flow. Presence. The state in which self-consciousness recedes and attention is fully absorbed. Mogi's exemplars are **children**, who "have no strong conception of past or future."

This pillar overlaps with Csikszentmihalyi's flow and Zen presence, but doesn't require a formal practice. The diagnostic question is simply: when do you lose time?

**Session exercises (Session 4 centerpiece):**
- "What activity makes you lose track of time?"
- "When was the last time you were fully here — not planning, not remembering, not performing?"
- "What conditions help that happen? What conditions prevent it?"

## Morita Therapy — The Sister Tradition

**Shōma Morita (1874–1938)** — Japanese psychiatrist, Zen-influenced. His psychotherapeutic approach, Morita therapy, is the intellectual cousin of ikigai and the antidote to the Western "you must love it" trap.

Central concept: **arugamama** (あるがまま) — *acceptance of things as they are*. Morita therapy does not try to eliminate uncomfortable feelings, fix anxiety, or "find passion first." It teaches purposeful action **despite** difficult feelings, rooted in *sei no yokubō* ("the will to live").

This matters for the skill because it licenses an important truth: **ikigai does not require that you feel excited about the activity.** A grandmother who has cared for grandchildren for ten years does not need to feel inspired each morning. The care is the ikigai; the feeling follows, sometimes, quietly.

**Session 7 uses arugamama directly:**
- "What are you currently waiting to feel ready for?"
- "What would you do *anyway*, regardless of how you feel about it today?"
- "Pick one tiny action you'd take even if motivation never arrives. Do it this week."
