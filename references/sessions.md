# The Seven Sessions

Each session is **15–30 minutes of real conversation**. The skill must never run multiple sessions in one invocation — the days between sessions are part of the practice.

Every session has the same shape:
1. **Recap** any carry-exercise from the previous session (or skip if this is Session 1).
2. **Run the dialogue.** Adapt, don't script.
3. **Append a journal entry** following [journal-template.md](journal-template.md).
4. **State the carry-exercise** — one concrete, small thing to do between now and next time.
5. **Close gently.** Remind them of `/ikigai check-in`.

---

## Session 1 — Orientation & Baseline

**Purpose:** Frame the authentic concept. Debunk the Venn diagram if the user has it in mind. Baseline Ikigai-9. Plant the Joy-of-Little-Things seed.

**References to load:** [authentic-concept.md](authentic-concept.md), [venn-critique.md](venn-critique.md), [ikigai-9.md](ikigai-9.md).

**Dialogue outline:**

1. **Open with the authentic framing** (short, conversational):
   > "Most of what's online about 'finding your ikigai' is based on a four-circle Venn diagram that a British guy made in 2014. It's not actually Japanese. Ken Mogi — the guy who wrote the bestselling book on ikigai — calls it 'rubbish.' What we're doing here is different. Ikigai in the Japanese sense is closer to: the specific thing, or things, that make your life feel worth living. It's often small. It's often plural. You already have some. This skill is going to help you notice them."

2. **Ask the Ohsaki question** literally:
   > "Before any frameworks, one question: *Do you have ikigai in your life?* Yes, no, uncertain. Whatever comes up."

   Listen. Don't rush to interpret. If they say no, that's the real starting point, not a failure. If they say yes, ask them to name one example and describe what it feels like.

3. **Administer the Ikigai-9** per [ikigai-9.md](ikigai-9.md). Record all nine item scores and total.

4. **Ask: what brought you here?**
   > "What made you want to do this now?"

   Listen for tone. Drift? Loss? Transition? Curiosity? Over-achievement fatigue? Record their own words.

5. **Plant the carry-exercise** — the 7-day Small Ikigai Log:
   > "Between now and next time, I'd like you to keep a small log. Once a day — morning, evening, whenever — note one small thing that felt like a reason to be alive today. Could be a cup of coffee. A bird. A text from someone. It doesn't have to be profound. Two sentences max. You can send it to me any time with `/ikigai check-in`, or just jot it yourself and bring the list to Session 2."

**Carry-exercise:** 7 daily entries in the Small Ikigai Log (one per day, ~2 sentences each).

**Journal entry:** starting state, Ikigai-9 baseline with all nine scores, the Ohsaki-question answer in their own words, what brought them here, the carry-exercise committed to.

## Session 2 — The Joy of Little Things

**Purpose:** Work deeply with the week of noticing. Sharpen the sensory texture. Map morning ritual.

**Mogi Pillar:** 4.

**References to load:** [mogi.md](mogi.md).

**Dialogue outline:**

1. **Review the week's log entries** out loud. If they missed days, fine — skip past it without comment. Read the ones they have, one by one.

2. **For each entry, ask one sharpening question:**
   - "What did you feel in your body at that moment?"
   - "If you had to describe the texture of that moment in one word, what is it?"
   - "What one sensory detail do you remember most?"

3. **Pattern-notice together:**
   > "Looking at all seven: what do you notice? Are they mostly solitary or social? Mostly morning or evening? Mostly sensory or relational?"

   Don't force a pattern. If there isn't one, that's fine.

4. **Map the morning ritual:**
   > "Walk me through the first 30 minutes after you wake up, in detail. Which second of it, if any, is a small ikigai?"

   If none: "what's one small thing you could add tomorrow — not an overhaul, a single detail — that might become one?"

5. **Micro-experiment carry-exercise.** Have them commit to *one* small addition or attention shift in their morning for the next several days. Keep the daily log going.

**Carry-exercise:** Keep the daily log. Plus one small morning addition (a specific, concrete act — not "be more present").

**Journal entry:** the 7-day log (append all entries), the patterns noticed, the morning ritual map, the new micro-experiment.

## Session 3 — Kodawari

**Purpose:** Locate where the user already shows obsessive care. Kodawari is a fingerprint of existing ikigai.

**Mogi Pillar:** 1.

**References to load:** [mogi.md](mogi.md).

**Dialogue outline:**

1. **Explain kodawari briefly:**
   > "There's a Japanese word, *kodawari* — it's when you apply more care to something than is strictly necessary. The craftsman who refines one broth for thirty years. The cook who tastes three times. It's a fingerprint of ikigai — wherever you already show it, ikigai is probably already there. The trick is to notice it. People rarely notice their own kodawari because it's so automatic."

2. **Ask, in order:**
   - "What activity do you do with more care than anyone would require of you?"
   - "What do you make that you wouldn't let ship half-done, even if no one would notice?"
   - "When you do that activity, what's the smallest detail you obsess over that others might not even see?"
   - "If no one ever praised you for it again, would you still do it that carefully?"

3. **Broaden:**
   - "Does kodawari show up in any of your relationships? (How you make someone's coffee, how you write a birthday message, how you listen.)"
   - "Does it show up in how you maintain something? (Tools, a garden, a car, a notebook, a journal.)"

4. **Challenge the lofty-answer reflex.** If the user answers with "excellence in my career" or something generalized, redirect:
   > "That's a shape of an answer, not an answer. What's the specific thing you refine, down to the detail?"

**Carry-exercise:** Two things this week — (1) Keep the daily log. (2) Do the thing you named, with even more attention than usual, and note what it feels like once.

**Journal entry:** Every kodawari the user named, in their own words. The specific details they obsess over. How it feels while doing it.

## Session 4 — Here and Now

**Purpose:** Find the flow states. Identify what conditions produce presence.

**Mogi Pillar:** 5.

**References to load:** [mogi.md](mogi.md).

**Dialogue outline:**

1. **Ask the core questions:**
   - "What activity makes you lose track of time?"
   - "When was the last time you were fully here — not planning, not remembering, not performing?"
   - "What were the conditions around that? (Alone or with others? Device on or off? Indoors or out? Rested or tired?)"

2. **If they struggle to name flow:** ask instead about childhood. "What did you lose hours to as a kid?" Childhood flow often survives as adult ikigai.

3. **Probe the inverse:**
   - "What reliably *prevents* that state for you? (Phone? Certain people? Certain kinds of work? Anxiety about something specific?)"

4. **Design a flow window:**
   > "This week, can you set up 45 minutes that looks like the conditions you just named? One time. Doesn't have to be productive. Doesn't have to be anything. Just re-create the conditions."

**Carry-exercise:** Keep the daily log + schedule and protect one 45-minute flow window before next session. Report back what happened.

**Journal entry:** Activities that produce flow, the conditions that support it, the conditions that kill it, the scheduled flow window.

## Session 5 — The Seven Needs Audit

**Purpose:** Use Kamiya's framework to get a wider-angle read on the user's current life.

**References to load:** [kamiya.md](kamiya.md).

**Dialogue outline:**

1. **Introduce the framework briefly:**
   > "Mieko Kamiya is the Japanese psychiatrist who founded ikigai psychology — the 1966 book *Ikigai ni Tsuite*. She identified seven needs that ikigai fulfills. We're going to walk through them. For each one, you rate 1 to 5 how well your current life meets it, and we talk about what's there."

2. **Walk through the seven needs** from [kamiya.md](kamiya.md). For each, use the diagnostic prompt in that file, then get the 1–5 rating.

3. **After all seven:**
   - Identify the two strongest. Ask: "what specific people, practices, or things correspond to those strengths?" These are likely where their existing ikigai lives. Name them.
   - Identify the two weakest. Ask: "is there anything about that reading that surprises you?" Then: "does any of this thinness feel chosen, or does it feel like drift?"

4. **Design one modest practice for each of the two weakest needs.** Emphasize *modest*. Kamiya specifically championed creativity in domestic/small pursuits. A 10-minute Sunday call to a parent (Resonance). A weekly cooking experiment (Self-Actualization). A morning walk without the phone (Freedom).

**Carry-exercise:** Keep the daily log + begin one of the two modest practices this week (user's choice which to start with).

**Journal entry:** All seven needs with 1–5 rating and the user's commentary. The two strongest with named sources. The two weakest with the chosen-vs-drift read. The two new modest practices.

## Session 6 — Life Review & Shift

**Purpose:** Acknowledge that ikigai shifts across life stages. Retrieve what has been (and what has ended). Honor transitions.

**References to load:** [kamiya.md](kamiya.md), [authentic-concept.md](authentic-concept.md).

**Dialogue outline:**

1. **Open directly:**
   > "Ikigai is not supposed to be fixed — it shifts as life stages shift. Today we're going to look at the arc. What's been, what is, what might be."

2. **Past:**
   - "What was your ikigai five years ago? Describe a specific thing that was giving your life meaning then. Is it still there?"
   - "What was your ikigai ten years ago? Same question."
   - "What has ended? What roles, practices, or relationships gave you meaning that no longer do? Is the ending mourned or complete?"

3. **Present (synthesis from prior sessions):**
   - "Based on what we've logged in Sessions 1 through 5 — what are the current sources?" Read back the user's own journal entries.

4. **Future:**
   - "What might be your ikigai five years from now? Don't predict — imagine. What seems possible?"
   - "What life-stage shift is already in motion that will change the picture?" (Retirement? A child leaving? A relationship forming? A health shift? A career ending or starting?)

5. **Integration:**
   > "Notice that the current sources are not permanent. Some will remain, some will fade, and new ones will arrive. Ikigai isn't something to 'achieve' and hold — it's something to keep noticing as the shape changes."

**Carry-exercise:** Keep the daily log + reach out to one person from a past chapter of your life. Not to rekindle — just to say hello. (Resonance + honoring what was.)

**Journal entry:** Past ikigai (5y, 10y), what has ended, present sources synthesized from journal, possible future ikigai, active life-stage shifts, the outreach planned.

## Session 7 — Arugamama & Tiny Experiments

**Purpose:** Commit to concrete, small, ongoing practices. Release the fantasy of "finding" ikigai in favor of *practicing* it.

**Morita principle:** arugamama (acceptance of things as they are; action despite feeling).
**Mogi Pillar:** 1 (Starting Small).

**References to load:** [mogi.md](mogi.md).

**Dialogue outline:**

1. **Name the shift:**
   > "We're not finishing today. We're starting. Ikigai isn't something you find once and keep. It's something you practice and notice, and the sources shift over time. Today we set up the practice."

2. **Arugamama check:**
   - "What are you currently waiting to feel ready for?"
   - "What would you do anyway — regardless of how motivated you feel — if 'ready' never arrived?"

3. **Tiny experiments.** Design **three small experiments** for the next four weeks, drawing from the journal so far:
   - **One Joy-of-Little-Things ritual** (daily, small, sensory — already in motion via the log; formalize it).
   - **One Kodawari practice** (weekly — pick the thing they named in Session 3, and commit to a specific dose of attention).
   - **One Resonance / Thin-Needs practice** (weekly — pick one of the two practices designed in Session 5 that isn't already active).

   Keep all three small. "Daily, 5 minutes" > "weekly, an hour." The constraint is Kamiya's freely-chosen constraint.

4. **Re-administer the Ikigai-9** per [ikigai-9.md](ikigai-9.md). Record all nine items and the total. Compare against Session 1 baseline. Note direction of change without making it a grade.

5. **Write the closing Patterns entry.** Read the full journal. Name 3–7 recurring sources (objects) and the recurring texture of their ikigai-kan (feeling). Use the user's own words.

6. **Invite return:**
   > "Invoke `/ikigai check-in` anytime to log a small ikigai. Invoke `/ikigai` after a season to re-audit the seven needs — ikigai shifts. You don't need to finish. You're already doing it."

**Carry-exercise (ongoing):** The three tiny experiments. The daily log. Check-ins as inspiration strikes.

**Journal entry:** The arugamama answers. The three tiny experiments with commit-level specificity. Ikigai-9 re-measure (all nine items). The Patterns synthesis. A final "closing note" in the user's own words if they want one.

---

## After Session 7

The skill is not "done." The user enters **ongoing mode** — daily check-ins, occasional return for re-audit. Specifically:

- `/ikigai check-in` remains available forever — one small ikigai logged per day, no session.
- `/ikigai status` shows progress, days since last log entry, total entries, Ikigai-9 trajectory.
- `/ikigai` (no arg) after 30+ days post-Session 7 triggers a **re-audit session** — redo the Seven Needs and Ikigai-9, update the journal, refresh the three tiny experiments.

This is how the practice becomes real.
